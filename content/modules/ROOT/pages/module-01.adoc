= Welcome to Super-Business

== The Situation

Welcome to your new role as a systems administrator at Super-Business! As you settle in on your first day, you notice an urgent email in your inbox.

[NOTE]
____
*From: Scott*

Subject: URGENT - Application Server Issue

Team,

I've been monitoring our server resources and noticed we were using a lot of disk space on the application server. To optimize our infrastructure, and maximize synergy, I provisioned a dedicated 5GB disk for our SuperBusinessApp and moved everything over to keep things organized.

The application is now running on `/app` and everything is working great! I made sure to size it appropriately for our current needs - no wasted space!

Let me know if you have any questions about my optimization work.

____

You check the timestamp - that email was sent two weeks ago. Before you can even think about it, another email arrives.

[NOTE]
____
*From: Scott*

Subject: RE: URGENT - Application Server Issue - CRITICAL UPDATE

Team,

The application has stopped working! It keeps saying "No space left on device" but that doesn't make sense because I just set this up with a brand new 5GB disk. The application can't possibly be using that much space already.

I need this fixed IMMEDIATELY. Our super-businessey business processes are being impacted!

-Scott
____

You sigh and check your other email. There's a message from Nate, the senior sysadmin.

[NOTE]
====
*From: Nate*

Subject: Welcome aboard!

Hey,

Welcome to the team! Sorry you're jumping right into the deep end on your first day.

I see Scott's been at it again. For context: two weeks ago he decided our application needed its own disk for "better organization." He created a 1GB filesystem because "that's all the app is using right now, why waste space?"

Of course, I tried to explain that applications grow and we should provision with headroom, but... well, you've read his emails.

The good news: Scott actually did provision a 5GB disk like he said. He just only created a 1GB filesystem on it and left the rest unused. Classic Scott move - bought a 5-bedroom house but only built 1 bedroom.

This should be a straightforward fix. The disk has plenty of space available - we just need to expand the logical volume and filesystem to use it. Think of it as finishing the construction on those other 4 bedrooms.

I've left some notes in my home directory that might help you out. See `/home/nate/lvm-notes.txt` for some reminders about the LVM expansion process.

Take your time, work methodically, and you'll have this sorted in no time. Scott's emails may be frantic, but there's no actual emergency - the app is just out of disk space, not broken.

-Nate

P.S. - Welcome to Super-Business. It's never boring around here.
====

== Your Mission

Your task is to investigate and resolve this disk space issue. You'll need to:

1. **Diagnose** the current state of the filesystem and LVM structure
2. **Analyze** the available resources and plan your expansion approach
3. **Resolve** the issue by expanding the logical volume and filesystem

This is a great learning opportunity to practice LVM expansion in a safe environment. The application isn't actually critical (despite what Scott says), so you can take your time and learn the proper procedure.

[TIP]
====
In the real world, running out of disk space on production systems is a common issue. Knowing how to safely expand LVM volumes is an essential skill for any systems administrator.

This lab will walk you through the systematic approach: diagnose first, understand what you have, plan your changes, then execute carefully.
====

== Getting Started

Let's begin by examining the system and understanding the current state. Click the **Terminal** tab and let's start our investigation!

[source,bash,role=execute]
----
ls -la /app
----

You should see the application directory structure. Now let's move on to the diagnosis phase where we'll examine the disk space situation in detail.
